Get to know the Bieszczady Mountains

Bieszczady Mountains is a part of Carpathian Mountain Range, situated at the southeast part of Poland. This is the wildest part of the country. The most beautiful part of the mountains is protected by the Bieszczady National Park and belongs to the Eastern Carpathian UNESCO Biosphere Reserve.

Bieszczady National Park

Is the third largest national park in Poland, located in Subcarpathian Voivodeship in the extreme southeast corner of the country, bordering Slovakia and Ukraine. Animal life is abundant with several species of endangered animals thriving in the area, among them brown bears, grey wolf, European wildcat, wild boar, European beavers, European otter, and European lynx as well as deer (such as moose) and European bison (over 500 live in the area).

Tarnica

Its height is 1,346 metres. It is one of the Polish Crown Peaks. The summit towers 500 metres above the Wołosatka Valley. It can be easily told apart from its neighbours by its distinctive shape. The mountain has two separate summits, one of 1,339 and one of 1,346 metres. The southern part is a steep rocky wall, while the other side consist of less steep rocky fields.

Solina

Lake Solina is an artificial lake in the Bieszczady Mountains region, more precisely in Lesko County of the Subcarpathian Voivodship of Poland. The lake was created in 1968 by the construction of the Solina Dam on the San River. It has an area of 22 square kilometres (8 sq mi) and contains 472,000,000 cubic metres (617,000,000 cu yd; 383,000 acre⋅ft) of water, making it Poland's largest artificial lake. It is the best known tourist attraction of the region to watersports enthusiasts. The lake's great depth, water clarity, and mountainous scenery makes it a very popular destination. The lake has been nicknamed the "Bieszczady Sea".

Greek Orthodox Churches

Centuries-old Greek Orthodox churches are a true landmark of the Bieszczady Mountains, with the majority and most interesting located in the area of Ustrzyki Dolne. Most are located within a 4 kilometre radius from each other, so they can be visited not only by car, but also by bike or on foot. There are 3 main architectural styles of those churches, inspired by Lemko, Bojko and Ukrainian cultures, with most of the temples having a separate additional wooden belfry.
